01
Overview & Problem

A therapy that works — if patients actually wear it.

tDCS is clinically validated for depression. The problem wasn't the science — it was the hardware. Existing devices looked clinical. Patients were embarrassed to use them at home. Adherence dropped. The therapy failed not because of efficacy but because of design.

The brief: build a device patients want to wear, that fits every head size, and works precisely enough for clinical use.

“If a patient is embarrassed to wear it, the therapy doesn't work — regardless of the engineering.”

Research & Insights

What patients and clinicians actually said.

Clinical interviews, competitive teardowns, and anthropometric head studies. Six findings shaped every design decision.

01

Stigma kills adherence

Existing devices read as “medical equipment.” Patients felt embarrassed using them at home.

02

Electrode contact is critical

Clinicians identified inconsistent F7/F8 contact as the #1 cause of ineffective therapy sessions.

03

One size fails everyone

At 95th percentile, the mastoid electrode pushed painfully against the ear.

04

Simplicity reduces errors

Every extra setup step increased the chance of incorrect use or abandonment for home users.

05

Discretion matters

Patients wanted to wear it without attracting attention at home. Form was a clinical variable.

06

LED feedback builds trust

Blink patterns for device states reduced anxiety about whether therapy was actually running.

04
Final Design

One size. Every head. No compromises.

Two engineering decisions resolve universal fit: a 5mm silicone overmoulded flex component at the top band, and a 3cm free-moving EEG PCB at the rear letting the mastoid ground electrode float and conform to different ear positions.

F7/F8 electrodes use a silicone flex element for consistent contact pressure. Ground contacts are conductive rubber — soft enough not to push against larger ears. RGB LED blink patterns communicate device state for home users.

05
Engineering Details & Electrode Assembly

Built to be assembled, not just rendered.

Internal routing channels carry the TDCS wire cleanly through the frame. The PCB and battery occupy a dedicated rear housing. The electrode stack — cellulose sponge / conductive rubber sheet / metal plate / electrode holder — clips in and is replaceable without tools.

TDCS wire routing & PCB housing (left) · electrode assembly: sponge → conductive rubber → metal plate → holder (right)

06
Usability Testing

Real heads revealed a real problem.

Testing on SLA-printed high-fidelity prototypes revealed a critical failure: at 95th percentile, the mastoid electrode was pushing against the ear. A shape and material revision was made before the final production run.

“Shape modifications and material re-selection were done for the mastoid ground electrode to comfortably fit a diverse population group.”

Post-revision testing confirmed comfortable fit and consistent electrode contact from 5th to 95th percentile without adjustment.

Usability testing on SLA-printed high-fidelity protos · 95th percentile (left) through 50th percentile (right)

07
Outcomes & Impact

From bench to clinical use — at scale.

150 units manufactured and deployed to clinical partners. Single-size solution simplified inventory. Patients describe it as “like a headphone.” The mastoid redesign eliminated the ear-pressure failure mode. Project remains active.
